How Honeybees Find Their Way Home

For a creature with a brain smaller than a grain of rice, the honeybee performs feats of navigation that continue to astonish scientists. A foraging bee may travel several kilometres from its hive in search of nectar, visiting dozens of flowers, yet it returns home by a near-straight path rather than retracing its winding outbound route.

Central to this ability is the bee's use of the sun as a compass. Even when the sun is hidden behind clouds, bees can detect the pattern of polarised light in patches of blue sky and infer the sun's position. Because the sun moves across the sky during the day, the bees' internal clock continually corrects for this movement, a process researchers call time compensation.

Distance is judged differently. Rather than counting wingbeats or measuring energy spent, a bee estimates how far it has flown by the speed at which the visual scene streams past its eyes — a cue known as optic flow. In a famous experiment, bees trained to fly through a narrow patterned tunnel overestimated the distance travelled, because the close walls made the scenery rush past more quickly.

Having found food, a returning bee shares its discovery through the celebrated "waggle dance". The angle of the dance relative to vertical encodes the direction of the food in relation to the sun, while the duration of the waggling run signals the distance. Remarkably, hive-mates can decode this information in complete darkness and fly directly to a source they have never seen.
